Using ghost zones for boundary conditions

In computational physics and engineering, especially in simulations involving partial differential equations (PDEs), boundary conditions play a crucial role in defining the behavior of the system at the boundaries of the computational domain. One common approach to implementing boundary conditions is through the use of ghost zones or ghost cells.

Concept:

The idea behind ghost zones is to extend the computational domain by adding additional grid points adjacent to the boundaries. These additional grid points, called ghost cells or ghost zones, are used to enforce boundary conditions without directly affecting the original domain.

Implementation:

When implementing ghost zones, the values of variables at the ghost cells are typically determined based on the specified boundary conditions. These values act as placeholders and are updated iteratively during the simulation to maintain consistency with the boundary conditions.

Types of Boundary Conditions:

Ghost zones can be used to implement various types of boundary conditions, including:

  • Dirichlet Boundary Conditions: In this case, the values at the ghost cells are set to specific prescribed values.
  • Neumann Boundary Conditions: Here, the values at the ghost cells are determined based on the derivative of the solution at the boundary.
  • Periodic Boundary Conditions: For periodic domains, the values at the ghost cells are obtained by wrapping around the values from the opposite boundary.
  • Robin Boundary Conditions: These conditions involve a combination of Dirichlet and Neumann conditions, where the values at the ghost cells are determined by a linear combination of the solution and its derivative at the boundary.

Advantages:

Using ghost zones for boundary conditions offers several advantages:

  • It allows for a consistent and systematic treatment of boundary conditions across different types of simulations.
  • It enables the use of standard numerical methods within the computational domain without modification.
  • It facilitates parallelization and optimization strategies by maintaining a regular grid structure.

Overall, ghost zones provide a flexible and efficient framework for incorporating boundary conditions in computational simulations, making them essential in various fields of scientific computing.

6th order finite differencing

The 6th order finite difference approximation for the second derivative \( f'' \) of a function \( f(x) \) is given by:

\[ f''(x) \approx \frac{-f(x + 3h) + 9f(x + 2h) - 45f(x + h) + 45f(x - h) - 9f(x - 2h) + f(x - 3h)}{h^2} \]

Similarly, the 6th order finite difference approximation for the first derivative \( f' \) of a function \( f(x) \) is:

\[ f'(x) \approx \frac{-\frac{1}{60}f(x + 3h) + \frac{3}{20}f(x + 2h) - \frac{3}{4}f(x + h) + \frac{3}{4}f(x - h) - \frac{3}{20}f(x - 2h) + \frac{1}{60}f(x - 3h)}{h} \]

Where \( h \) is the step size between the discrete points.

4th order Runge-Kutta methods

The Runge-Kutta 4th order (RK4) method is a numerical technique used to solve ordinary differential equations (ODEs). It provides a balance between accuracy and computational complexity, making it one of the most popular methods for solving ODEs numerically.

The general form of the RK4 method for solving an initial value problem with a first-order ODE is:

\[ y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4) \]

Where:

  • \( y_n \) is the approximation of the solution at time \( t_n \).
  • \( h \) is the step size.
  • \( k_1, k_2, k_3, \) and \( k_4 \) are slopes determined at different points using the function \( f(t, y) \) representing the ODE.

The algorithm is as follows:

  1. Compute \( k_1 \) using the function \( f(t_n, y_n) \).
  2. Compute \( k_2 \) using the function \( f(t_n + \frac{h}{2}, y_n + \frac{h}{2}k_1) \).
  3. Compute \( k_3 \) using the function \( f(t_n + \frac{h}{2}, y_n + \frac{h}{2}k_2) \).
  4. Compute \( k_4 \) using the function \( f(t_n + h, y_n + hk_3) \).
  5. Update the solution \( y_{n+1} \) using the weighted average of the slopes \( k_1, k_2, k_3, \) and \( k_4 \).

The RK4 method offers relatively high accuracy compared to simpler methods like Euler's method, making it suitable for a wide range of applications in science and engineering.

GALACTIC DYNAMO SIMULATION WITH RADIAL INFLOW (1D in r)

ABSTRACT

Dynamo theory explains the sustained generation and evolution of magnetic fields across cosmic timescales. Mean-field dynamo theory offers insights into the presence and structure of large-scale magnetic fields in disc galaxies. This study investigates the influence of radial inflow, a prevalent phenomenon in such discs, on dynamo activity in the kinematic regime. The objective is to determine whether radial inflows amplify or attenuate dynamo action. Three different velocity profiles and a case without considering any inflows were studied and compared to reach a conclusion. Contrary to expectations, findings demonstrate that radial motions impede rather than enhance dynamo activity. Specifically, the dynamo struggles to replenish the magnetic field transported by radial flows away from regions of efficient generation, thereby inhibiting its effectiveness. This study is done for a standard \(\alpha-\Omega\) dynamo, and equations are solved one dimensional in \(r\). Thin disk approximation and no-\(z\) approximations are used, and disk flaring is not considered.

INTRODUCTION

Dynamo theory traces its origins back to pioneering works by Parker (1971a) and Vainshtein and Ruzmaikin (1971), who proposed mean-field dynamo models to explain the generation of magnetic fields in spiral galaxies. Dynamos play a crucial role in shaping the magnetic environment of various astrophysical systems, including galaxies, stars, and accretion disks. Understanding dynamo mechanisms is essential for deciphering cosmic magnetism, star formation, and galactic evolution.

In galactic mean-field theory, the evolution of magnetic fields is comprehensively described by the induction equation, which plays a pivotal role in understanding the dynamics of these fields within cosmic structures. The induction equation, fundamental to magnetohydrodynamics (MHD), explains how magnetic fields evolve under the influence of various physical processes. The \(\alpha-\Omega\)dynamo model represents a cornerstone in galactic dynamo theory, offering a robust framework for studying large-scale magnetic fields in spiral galaxies. In this model, the mean magnetic field evolution is governed by the combined effects of turbulent \(\alpha\)-effect and differential rotation,\(\Omega\)-effect. Mathematically, the mean-field dynamo equation takes the form: \[\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} = \nabla \times (\mathbf{V} \times \mathbf{B}) + \nabla \times (\alpha \mathbf{B}) - \nabla \times (\eta \nabla \times \mathbf{B})\] Where \(\mathbf{B}\) represents the mean magnetic field, \(\mathbf{V}\) is the velocity field, \(\alpha\) denotes the turbulent diffusivity, and \(\eta\) is the magnetic diffusivity. This formalism captures the dynamic interplay between fluid motion and magnetic field amplification, providing insights into galactic magnetic field evolution over cosmic timescales.

The induction equation comprises two key components: one governing the evolution of the radial magnetic field (\( \overline{B}_r \)) and the other dictating the evolution of the azimuthal magnetic field (\( \overline{B}_{\phi} \)). These equations are generally expressed in cylindrical coordinates (\( r, \phi, z \)). The evolution of the radial and azimuthal magnetic fields is described by the following equations: \[ \frac{\partial \overline{B}_r}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(\alpha \overline{B}_{\phi}) - \frac{\overline{V}_r}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\overline{B}_r) + \eta_t \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\overline{B}_r)\right) + \frac{\partial^2 \overline{B}_r}{\partial z^2} \right] \] \[ \frac{\partial \overline{B}_{\phi}}{\partial t} = -q\Omega \overline{B}_r - \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(\overline{V}_r\overline{B}_{\phi}) + \eta_t \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\overline{B}_{\phi})\right) + \frac{\partial^2 \overline{B}_{\phi}}{\partial z^2} \right] \] Here, we apply no-\(z\) approximation to the derivatives in \(z\).

Radial inflow, a prevalent phenomenon in galactic discs, significantly influences dynamo activity. The advection of magnetic fields by inflowing material alters the magnetic field distribution, impacting its generation and evolution. In the \(\alpha\Omega\) dynamo framework, the effect of radial inflow on magnetic field advection is encapsulated in the induction equation, contributing to the overall dynamics of galactic magnetism. That is, in short, radial inflows tend to suppress dynamo action.

When there's no significant magnetic field extending to infinity (which is necessary for a dynamo system), the dynamo must constantly replenish the magnetic field carried away by the radial flow from where it's generated most efficiently. This makes it harder for the dynamo to sustain itself. Additionally, the compression of the magnetic field, described by the term  \(B(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v})\) in the induction equation, doesn't counterbalance this effect. Instead, it leads to a sharper peak in magnetic field strength at smaller radii. In simpler terms, the inflow of material away from where the magnetic field is generated makes it challenging for the dynamo to maintain the magnetic field, and the compression of the magnetic field only makes the situation more pronounced by concentrating the field strength towards the center.

METHODS

The simplified numerical model

The dimensionless equations used are in accordance with D. Moss et al. (2000). It is as follows:

\[ \frac{\partial B_r}{\partial t} = -\lambda Rv \frac{v_r}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} (B_r r) - R\alpha B_\phi - B_r + \lambda^2 \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left[ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} (r B_r) \right] \] \[ \frac{\partial B_\phi}{\partial t} = -\lambda Rv \frac{\partial}{\partial r} (v_r B_\phi) + R\omega r B_r \frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial r} - B_\phi + \lambda^2 \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left[ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} (r B_\phi) \right] \] For \(\Omega\), we used the Brandt rotation law: \[\Omega=\frac{\Omega_0}{(1+(r/r_{\omega})^2)^{1/2}}\]

These equations represent the time evolution of the magnetic field components \(B_r\) and \(B_\phi\) in a cylindrical coordinate system.

The value of \(r_\omega\) is set to be 0.2R, where R is the endpoint radius used to make length scales dimensionless. The dynamo numbers are \[ R_\alpha = \frac{\alpha_0 h_0}{\eta}, \, R_\omega = \frac{h^2 \Omega_0}{\eta}, \, R_v = \frac{v_0 h}{\eta} \]

Boundary conditions \(B_r=B_{\phi}=0\) are applied on the outer boundary of \(r_f=15.5 \, \text{kpc}\) as well as inner boundary \(r_i=0.01 \, \text{kpc}\), which is 1 in the dimensionless case (chosen to avoid "division by zero" and "overflow" errors).

In this study, we only consider the axisymmetric solutions, i.e., the \(\phi\) derivatives are not considered. We consider three different velocity profiles, namely:

  • Profile 1: \( v_r = \sin(\pi r) \)
  • Profile 2: \( v_r = \begin{cases} -\sin\left(\frac{\pi r}{r_\omega}\right), & r \leq r_\omega \\ \sin\left[\frac{\pi(r - r_\omega)}{1 - r_\omega}\right], & r > r_\omega \end{cases} \)
  • Profile 3: \( v_r = \begin{cases} -\sin\left(\frac{\pi r}{r_\omega}\right), & r \leq r_\omega \\ 0, & r > r_\omega \end{cases} \)

By keeping the value of \(R_v\) to 1 (i.e., \(v_0\) about 0.7 km/s), we run the codes for all three profiles and compare the results. The code is automated to run until the global growth rate is attained. We first determined the global growth rate in the linear regime, \(\Gamma\), for \(R_v\) set at 1. Next, for velocity profile 1, we varied \(R_v\) from -3 to +3, again determining the global growth rates. We used \(R_{\alpha}=1, R_{\omega}=10\), and \(\lambda=0.05\). The number of total spatial steps was 50, with 10,000-time steps per unit of time, but these dramatic changes in resolution did not affect results. The variation of the pitch angle is also studied.

Numerical methods implemented

For this task, we implemented the following numerical methods:

  • Finite differencing: 6th-order finite differencing. We employ a 6th-order finite differencing scheme to calculate spatial derivatives numerically. The scheme utilizes the values of the function at nearby grid points to approximate the derivative at each point. The scheme approximates the spatial derivatives of a function \(f(x)\) using values of \(f\) at nearby grid points. The central difference formula for the 6th order scheme can be expressed as: \[ \frac{{df}}{{dx}}(x_i) \approx \frac{1}{60h} \left(-\frac{147}{10}f(x_{i-3}) + 6f(x_{i-2}) - \frac{15}{2}f(x_{i-1 }) + \frac{20}{3}f(x_{i}) - \frac{15}{2}f(x_{i+1}) + 6f(x_{i+2}) - \frac{147}{10}f(x_{i+3})\right) \] Where: - \(f(x_i)\) is the value of the function \(f\) at grid point \(x_i\), - \(h\) is the spatial step size, - \(x_{i-3}, x_{i-2}, \ldots, x_{i+3}\) are neighboring grid points.
  • Runge-Kutta integration: The fourth-order Runge-Kutta method is a numerical technique for solving ordinary differential equations (ODEs). It approximates the solution at the next time step \(t_{n+1}\) based on the solution at the current time step \(t_n\). The RK4 method consists of four stages (k1, k2, k3, k4) for each time step, and the update formula is given by: \[ \begin{align*} k_1 &= hf(t_n, y_n) \\ k_2 &= hf(t_n + \frac{1}{2}h, y_n + \frac{1}{2}k_1) \\ k_3 &= hf(t_n + \frac{1}{2}h, y_n + \frac{1}{2}k_2) \\ k_4 &= hf(t_n + h, y_n + k_3) \\ y_{n+1} &= y_n + \frac{1}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4) \end{align*} \] Where: - \(t_n\) and \(t_{n+1}\) are the current and next time steps, respectively, - \(y_n\) is the approximate solution at time \(t_n\), - \(h\) is the time step size, - \(f(t_n, y_n)\) is the derivative of the function \(y(t)\) with respect to \(t\) evaluated at time \(t_n\) and \(y_n\), - \(k_1\), \(k_2\), \(k_3\), and \(k_4\) are intermediate slopes.
  • Implementation of boundary conditions: The ghost zone handling technique is critical to numerical simulations, especially when employing finite difference schemes to solve partial differential equations (PDEs) on a discrete grid. In our simulation, we utilize various strategies to handle ghost zones, which are additional grid points introduced beyond the physical domain to accommodate boundary conditions and ensure accurate numerical computations near the boundaries. The simulations were run using symmetric, anti-symmetric, and relative anti-symmetric ghost zones, and anti-symmetric ghost zones were found to be good at keeping both boundaries at 0. Anti-symmetric boundary conditions enforce anti-symmetry in the fields across the boundary. This is achieved by reflecting the values of the fields about the boundary and negating them, ensuring that the fields exhibit anti-symmetric behavior near the boundaries.
  • Automation of the code to get global decay rate The local growth rate is calculated at each unit diffusion time; we also calculate the mean of these values and continuously check if their differences come under a specific tolerance value set at 0.001. If the values come under tolerance, we are already in the global growth rate stage. To enhance precision, there is a patience limit, i.e., three more steps, and if the mean value is still under tolerance, the code is stopped.

Pitch angle evolution

For the pitch angle evolution, we plotted the pitch angle at the last time step for all three velocity profiles. The results are shown in figures 21,22 and 23. Key findings

The key findings from this study are as follows:

  1. Contrary to initial expectations, the study demonstrates that radial inflows hinder dynamo action rather than enhance it. Radial inflows in the galactic disc can disrupt the local conditions necessary for generating and maintaining magnetic fields. Magnetic fields are typically generated in regions where differential rotation causes twisting and amplification of the field lines. However, radial inflows can disrupt this process by transporting magnetic fields away from these regions before they can fully amplify, thereby hindering dynamo action. The presence of radial inflows thus makes it challenging for the dynamo to sustain magnetic fields by constantly advecting them from regions of efficient generation.
  2. Radial inflows introduce additional turbulence and mixing within the galactic disc, which can disrupt the ordered motion required for dynamo action. This turbulence can scatter and diffuse the magnetic fields, reducing their coherence and inhibiting the amplification process essential for dynamo activity.
  3. The growth rate of the dynamo is maximized for profile 3, closely resembling the growth rate in the absence of inflows.
  4. The results we obtained are in good agreement with the literature, but in TABLE 2, the case of \(R_v=-3\) is an anomaly. However, this does not change our inference because we have enough evidence from the field evolution and growth rate curves.
  5. As the absolute value of \(R_v\) increases, the growth rate decreases, indicating that the sign of this dynamo number has a significant impact on dynamo activity.
  6. The pitch angle evolution remains almost constant for all cases, suggesting that the pitch angle is not significantly affected by radial inflows.
  7. This suggests that while radial inflows may affect the strength and coherence of magnetic fields, they have minimal impact on their overall alignment within the galactic disc.

CONCLUSIONS

  • In this study, we investigated the influence of radial inflow on dynamo activity within galactic discs, particularly focusing on the behavior of large-scale magnetic fields in the presence of different velocity profiles. Our findings suggest that radial inflows impede rather than enhance dynamo action, as evident from figures 3, 4, and 5.
  • Radial inflows tend to suppress dynamo activity by transporting magnetic fields away from regions of efficient generation. This disrupts the replenishment of magnetic fields, making it challenging for the dynamo to sustain itself. The magnetic field compression further exacerbates this effect, leading to a concentration of magnetic field strength towards the galactic center.
  • We compared 3 different velocity profiles with sinusoidal dependence with radius and a case without considering any inflows; the results are in Tables 2 and 3. We found that we get a higher growth when the radial inflow is less pronounced. We also concluded that the absolute value of dynamo number \(R_v\) is inversely proportional to the growth rate. The animations for growth rate in figures 18, 19, 20, and 21 support this conclusion.
  • The results agree with the literature, but we have some unexpected values in Table 3; this might be due to some value error during implementation.
  • \(R_v\) represents the ratio of the advection timescale to the diffusion timescale within the fluid medium where the dynamo operates. When the absolute value of \(R_v\) increases, diffusion becomes more dominant than advection. In this scenario, magnetic fields are more effectively diffused away from regions of generation, hindering their amplification and resulting in a lower dynamo growth rate.
  • Pitch angle evolution is relatively constant as found from figures 21, 22, and 23. The stability of the galactic disc structure likely limits significant changes in magnetic field alignment despite radial inflows introducing turbulence. Other dominant factors, such as galactic and differential rotation, may overshadow the effects of radial inflows on magnetic field alignment. Conservation laws may contribute to maintaining the overall alignment of magnetic fields within the galactic disc.
  • Changes in pitch angle due to radial inflows may occur over longer time scales, making them relatively minor and not readily observable within the study's timeframe.

Limitations

  • Because of high run times, the spatial resolution was compromised to optimize time resolution and thus run time.
  • Solving in only one dimension can be limiting; more interesting results and physics can be obtained in 2D.
  • Because of the long run times to make gif files, the fps was compromised, thus the less smooth transitions.
  • Probing into the non-linear regime.

Improvements and future scope

  • Incorporating more realistic parameters into the model.
  • Solving the dynamo equation in 2D.
  • Implementing more advanced and accurate numerical methods.
  • Incorporating disk flaring scenario and using alpha-quenching mechanisms.
  • Probing into the non-linear regime.
